🔗 Introduction

This unit focuses on the architecture of knowledge-based systems and how the various concepts discussed so far fit together. It introduces further reasoning concepts and discusses critical issues that ontology developers and knowledge-based system professionals must be aware of — including Close and Open World Assumptions in Description Logic.

🎯 This Week We Shall Learn About
  • The architecture of knowledge-based systems and its role in KRR
  • The concepts of Attribute, Language and Complements
  • Close and Open World Assumptions in Description Logic
On Completion of This Week, You Will Be Able To
  • Develop further understanding of formalism approaches and techniques in KRR
  • Appreciate the challenges involved in applying KRR techniques
  • Develop awareness of issues around KRR-based systems

Reply to Lauretta Oghenevurie

Dear Lauretta, thank you for taking the time to read my initial post and for such an insightful reply. You raise valid points about the practical deployment challenges of OWL 2 in large-scale agent systems.

OWL 2's effectiveness depends on its implementation approach, not just theory. Highlighting OWL 2 profiles is important — they let developers balance expressiveness and computational efficiency for web-scale applications. Separating ontological reasoning from querying aligns with modern semantic architecture: using OWL 2 for reasoning, SPARQL for queries, and SWRL or RIF for rules allows agents to benefit from advanced reasoning whilst specialised query engines ensure performance.

I would argue that this modular flexibility is precisely what makes OWL 2 valuable — its ability to integrate within broader semantic stacks whilst providing standardised ontological foundations. The profile-driven approach you mention does not diminish OWL 2's usefulness but rather demonstrates its adaptability to varying application requirements (Krötzsch, 2012).

— Andrea

Reply to Matthew Bullen

Dear Matthew, thank you for reading my initial post and for your interesting reply. Your introduction of Doing and Wisnesky's perspective on data migration and integration adds an important practical dimension to our discussion.

Your perspective of treating ontology implementation as data integration rather than just semantic description is compelling. Handling varied data sources with incompatible or missing schemas is a crucial practical issue that goes beyond OWL 2's theory. Your focus on embedding translation rules within the ontology fits well with OBDA methods, where mapping languages like R2RML connect relational databases to RDF/OWL.

This supports rather than challenges OWL 2 — its standardised framework serves as a reliable semantic base for integration. OWL 2's formal semantics combined with practical data integration illustrate modular semantic architecture (Poggi et al., 2008): OWL 2 supplies the ontological foundation, while other technologies address data transformation and schema alignment, allowing for both semantic precision and flexibility.

Do you see potential challenges in maintaining logical consistency when dynamically integrating diverse data sources?

— Andrea
