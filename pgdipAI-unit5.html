Welcome to Week 5.

Cluster analysis or clustering is the task of grouping a set of objects in such a way that objects in the same group (called a cluster) are more similar (in some sense) to each other than to those in other groups (clusters).

It is a main task of exploratory data analysis, and being used in many fields, including pattern recognition, image analysis, information retrieval, bioinformatics, data compression, computer graphics and machine learning.

This week we shall learn about:

  • Basic idea behind clustering.
  • Different techniques of distance measurements.
  • K-means and agglomerative clustering.
  • How to evaluate clusters.

On completion of this week, you will be able to:

  • Understand the logic which underpins clustering.
  • Identify skill sets required to evaluate the results of cluster analysis.
  • Understand the pitfalls of clustering techniques.

Personal Reflection

Clustering is a fundamental unsupervised learning technique that reveals hidden patterns and structures within unlabeled data, making it essential for exploratory data analysis and knowledge discovery.

Unlike supervised learning, clustering algorithms like K-means, hierarchical clustering, and DBSCAN identify natural groupings without predefined categories, enabling practitioners to understand data distributions and uncover meaningful segments.

This capability is crucial for numerous applications including customer segmentation, market research, image recognition, and anomaly detection.

Clustering serves as a preprocessing step for other machine learning tasks, helping to reduce dimensionality, improve model performance, and identify outliers that might skew results.

Understanding clustering algorithms develops critical analytical thinking about data relationships and similarity measures.

"Clustering teaches practitioners to evaluate cluster quality, determine optimal cluster numbers, and interpret results meaningfully.

These skills are particularly valuable in business intelligence, where discovering customer behaviors or market segments can drive strategic decisions and competitive advantages in data-driven organizations."
