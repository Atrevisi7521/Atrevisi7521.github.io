Unit 9: Introduction to Convolutional Neural Networks

Welcome to Week 9.

Deep learning has revolutionised artificial intelligence by enabling highly complex pattern recognition across different domains, from image processing to natural language understanding. This week, we will build upon traditional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and explore more advanced architectures such as:

  • Dueling Neural Networks (used in Reinforcement Learning & GANs).
  • Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), which power image synthesis, deepfake technology, and AI-driven creativity.
  • Transformer models (BERT, GPT, T5), which have redefined NLP by replacing recurrent networks (RNNs & LSTMs).

    • This week we shall learn about:

    • TThe algorithm underpinning CNN in detail.
    • CNN specific python libraries and examples.
    • Applications of CNN in real life.

    On completion of this week, you will be able to:

    • Understand the application and importance of computer vision.
    • Undertake basic computer vision related tasks.

    Reply to Dalbir Singh's initial post

    Hi Dalbir,

    Thanks for your initial post which I found remarkably interesting. I have to say that I agree with your conclusions about the AI writers, I am also concerned about the usage of those tools for creative tasks (i.e. poems) and for the risks of bias or disinformation.

    I was indeed reading some books and publications about AI writing generating tools and I found that many people, especially students, are also concerned about the creative usage of those tools for academic purposes.

    But an interesting and widely shared concern comes from professional writers and is about ethical issues that could arise from the usage of AI tools. As reported by Higgs and Stornaiuolo (2024: 642) “quite a bit of conversation revolved around the threats of AI to creative enterprise more broadly, whether that involved professional writers or artists, concerns over ‘deepfakes’, or worries about how ChatGPT dataset was trained on people’s texts without consent.”

    I think I can say that I agree with you when you affirm that for admin or mechanical task, AI writing generating tools are helpful but when it comes to creativity and truth, those tools still need human interaction and control to avoid issues like the ones mentioned above.

    Reply to Choyee Wolff's initial post

    Hi Choyee,

    Thanks for your initial post which I enjoyed reading.

    I found interesting your focus on the educational and professional setting of the usage of the AI generative writing tools. I think that, for the professional side, the usage of those tools could be useful to save some time in performing mechanical and automated tasks or for some basic mail replies.

    But the educational and academic usage of AI writing tools deserves a bit more of attention from an ethical perspective. A particularly crucial point is not to let the students alone in this new AI environment. Here is important the figure of the teachers or professors and their stands on the AI writing tools.

    As wisely reported, talking about ChatGPT usage, by Črček and Patekar (2023: 135) “while the tool is still relatively new, and a hotly debated topic, instructors’ opinions are key to setting the foundations for AI usage in classrooms. If there are no guidelines on what for and how to use ChatGPT, it cannot be expected of students to figure out the ethical usage and to solve the issue of academic honesty by themselves.”

    Can we agree on the fact that the usage of AI writing tools for academic or creative writing should be regulated or, at least, supervised by humans ?

    Personal Reflection

    Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) has been quite a challenging topic to handle. But I learned that is a fundamental tool to learn. EDA is a crucial preliminary step in any AI/machine learning pipeline that involves:

    Understanding the structure and characteristics of your data before building AI models

    Identifying patterns, trends, and relationships in the dataset

    Detecting outliers, missing values, and data quality issues

    Determining which features might be most relevant for your AI model

    I am really looking forward to start working practically on some datasets and try the Exploratory Data Analysis myself.

    "EDA is like getting to know your data intimately before you ask it to power an AI system - it's the foundation that makes everything else more reliable and effective."
