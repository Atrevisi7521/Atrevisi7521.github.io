Reply to Fabian Narel

Dear Fabian,

Thank you for your post, which provides a thoughtful overview of the ethical challenges surrounding generative AI.

I appreciated your focus on transparency and accountability, two issues that are increasingly important in discussions about AI governance.

Your reference to the EU AI Act is timely, the move towards greater transparency in general-purpose AI models represents a substantial regulatory development. Of course, implementation will be complex, as Veale and Zuiderveen Borgesius (2021) point out. The idea of mandatory documentation for training data is aligned with best practices, although it raises significant questions, especially regarding how to document billions of data points while keeping the process both transparent and computationally feasible.

The ‚ÄúStochastic Parrots‚Äù paper that you referenced is foundational to this field. I would also add that bias can arise not only from training data, but also from design choices and deployment contexts (Abebe et al., 2020). Human oversight is crucial, but there is a risk of automation bias, where humans may over-rely on AI suggestions without sufficient scrutiny.

One point I would like to discuss further is the challenge of deepfakes and misinformation. How do we verify the authenticity of content online while safeguarding privacy rights and freedom of expression? This tension is significant, particularly within the context of the transparency requirements you highlighted.

Do you think the EU‚Äôs regulatory approach could set a global standard, or will regional differences in AI governance lead to fragmentation?
