Reply to Eslam Salaheldin Abdelnaser Abdelhafez

Hi Eslam, thank you for taking the time to read my initial post and reply with so many interesting points for discussion.

It is clear that KR did not emerge suddenly with Artificial Intelligence — as we read in Professor Weststeijn's article, it has far more ancient origins. Doing some further research, I found a very interesting work by Slaughter (1982: 11) highlighting how both natural historians and linguists tried to develop language systems where objects could be simultaneously named and described.

In my opinion, KR is not a recent phenomenon — but it clearly has a new focus with the arrival of Artificial Intelligence. The key question today is whether the computational aspect of KR adds something fundamentally new to the discipline, or whether it simply adapts traditional KR concepts to the new environment of AI.

Fikes and Garvey (2020: 9) noted that the development of KRR has been motivated by new challenging questions, the complexity of expanding subject areas, and improvements in computational technology. I would love to hear your further thoughts on this.

— Andrea

Reply to Gayathridevi Durairaj

Hi Gayathridevi, thank you for your thoughtful post. I agree that while computational KR is modern, its conceptual foundations are historically grounded. Your use of Weststeijn's work effectively demonstrates early systematic knowledge encoding attempts.

You highlighted that "without reasoning, KR remains limited to data organisation and retrieval" (Durairaj, 2025) — and I'd like to build on that, because I think it is a very powerful starting point for debate. I agree, and as Davis et al. (1993: 29) put it: "representation and reasoning are inextricably intertwined: We cannot talk about one without also unavoidably discussing the other."

But I want to push this further: is KR without reasoning simply limited — or does it cease entirely to be Knowledge Representation? If we continue reading Davis et al. (1993: 25), they define Knowledge Representation itself as a theory of intelligent reasoning. In that case, reasoning is not merely complementary to KR — it is the very reason KR exists. The relationship is not just a connection, but something far more fundamental.

I would love to continue analysing this fascinating relationship between Knowledge Representation and Reasoning.

— Andrea
